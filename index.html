Jesper Vestin's
Employment

A Dedicated Servant of the Working World

2021  —  2026

A Message from Jesper

Before you read what follows, I want to say something directly.

I genuinely enjoyed working with you. All of you. Most of you. The vast majority of you.

You were the reason I never dreaded coming in. On the days that were long, complicated, or just a bit much, it was the people around me that made them worth showing up for. From the jokes in the office to the laughs we somehow managed after a hectic hotel opening, you made the hard parts enjoyable, the frustrating parts manageable, and the good moments genuinely good.

This is the end of my Strawberry Journey.

For now.

Jesper

After five years of distinguished, occasionally heroic, and largely unsung service, the employment of Jesper Vestin departed this world peacefully in 2026, surrounded by half-finished Nocco cans, an inbox it had finally stopped apologising for, and the quiet satisfaction of a desk cleared with something resembling dignity.

Born of necessity in 2021, the employment quickly established itself as the kind of presence that made an entire company function more smoothly than it had any right to. As IT Support, it travelled, translated, and troubleshot its way across departments and head offices alike, becoming the first person most people called when something went wrong, and, more importantly, the person they called when they were not quite sure who else to call.

Over the years it became something more than a support function. A translator between "it doesn't work" and "it works now." A calm voice in rooms where panic was the default setting. The kind of colleague who showed up in person, fixed the thing, explained the fix in a way that actually made sense, and left before anyone fully appreciated how serious the problem had been.

(This was, in retrospect, a choice.)

It endured countless software migrations, multiple reorganisations, and enough "quick syncs" to fill several lifetimes. It answered the 07:43 Google Chat message. It joined the Friday wrap-up it suspected no one truly needed. And it documented things. Properly. In full sentences. A practice so rare in its field that colleagues occasionally printed the documentation and kept it.

And, on most occasions, it was proud of that.

"It never took all of its allocated vacation days.
Not once. Not even close."

In its later years, the employment grew more deliberate in its assessments, more precise with its words, and increasingly skilled at delivering unwelcome news in a tone so agreeably neutral that the full weight of the message only landed several minutes later, over coffee, when it was too late to argue.

Colleagues remember it as thorough, direct in a way that occasionally required a brief moment of adjustment, and possessed of a gift for making complicated things feel manageable. It was the person people connected with early. Not because it sought it out, but because it was genuinely, consistently, and sometimes exhaustingly helpful.

In its final weeks, those closest to the employment noted a certain serenity. A lightness. Tasks were closed. Systems were handed over. Documentation, against all odds, was written.

And somewhere in that process, a quiet shift occurred. What had once been a role became a chapter.

It is survived by improved systems, fixed things that no longer need fixing, and a noticeable absence in Google Chat threads where it once replied first.

Also, one charger.
Its whereabouts remain a matter of personal conscience.

Survived By

  • A LinkedIn profile, recently updated and suspiciously optimistic
  • A handover document of almost alarming thoroughness
  • The institutional memory of an entire department, now distributed across no one in particular
  • Several colleagues who will, in approximately six months, finally understand what it was doing
  • A recurring calendar invite it will no longer attend, but which will persist regardless
  • A Google Chat thread, now noticeably quieter
  • An out-of-office message, composed with care and a hint of theatrical flair
  • One mug, location unknown, presumed gone

Notice

No formal services will be held, as the deceased would have found them unnecessary and slightly awkward. In lieu of flowers, mourners are asked to update their contact information and resist the urge to send a final "just checking in" message. The work inbox has been left on read. It will stay that way.
